Proof

Let \(V, E, F\) denote the number of vertices, edges, and faces of \(\bfG\text{.}\) Since every face is a hexagon or pentagon, we have \(E=P+H\text{,}\) and substituting this into Euler's theorem gives:

\begin{equation} V-E+P+H=2\tag{3.5.1} \end{equation}

Now we turn handshaking. Since \(\bfG\) is trivalent, every vertex has degree three, and so vertex-edge handshaking becomes:

\begin{equation} 2E=3V\tag{3.5.2} \end{equation}

Finally, since there are \(P\) pentagonal faces and \(H\) hexagonal faces, face-edge handshaking becomes:

\begin{equation} 2E=5P+6H\tag{3.5.3} \end{equation}

Multiplying (3.5.1) by six gives:

\begin{equation*} 6V-6E+6P+6H=12 \end{equation*}

Multiplying (3.5.2) by two gives \(6V-4E=0\text{,}\) which we can use to eliminate \(V\text{,}\) giving

\begin{equation*} -2E+5P+6H=12 \end{equation*}

Finally, using (3.5.3) we can eliminate both \(E\) and \(H\) in one go, being left with \(P=12\) as desired.
